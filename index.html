Marking as Dead
When a lead is done, mark it Dead the right way. The CRM uses one action with one required dropdown — pick the correct dead reason every time so the pipeline stays clean.
How to mark Dead in InvestorFuse: On the lead, open "Select the next action" → choose "Mark as dead." A second dropdown appears: "Select dead reason." Pick the correct reason from the options below. Always leave a short note on the lead describing exactly what happened on the call.
The big distinction — qualified vs. not qualified. Before picking a Dead reason, ask: was this lead ever qualified? If the lead never qualified (we never really had a shot), use one of the top-level reasons: Not Interested · Do Not Call · Wrong Number · Invalid. If the lead WAS qualified — they fit our buy box, real motivation, we'd been working it — but the deal slipped away, use Opportunity Lost with the right sub-reason. Same word ("Not Interested") can appear in both places — what differs is whether the lead was already qualified before it died.

Not interested
Sr. ACQ
When to use it

The seller still owns property, the number could work, and they're not a DNC — but the opportunity is over. One of the four situations below applies.

Sub-reasons (write the specific one in your note)
  • Not selling anymore — circumstances changed, they're keeping the property / off the market.
  • Blocked — you've tried calling multiple times and it never rings. Strong signal they blocked the number.
  • Fuck off — seller was hostile, cursed you out, told you off. Do not push further.
  • Too high on price — seller's number is so far above market that there's no deal path, even after a re-engage. Not a "we're apart" situation — a "no deal ever" situation.

Legal-risk territory. Use this when:

Driving toward decision

"If you don't guide the deal to a decision, it will stall." Stop having good conversations. Start getting commitments.
The objective

A great call is not when the seller is happy and asking questions. A great call is when the seller makes a decision — YES or NO. The biggest mistake closers make is letting sellers drift. "This sounds great, I'll follow up" is a stall with good vibes attached to it.

Weak vs. shark closers
Weak closer
  • Talks too much, loses control of the call
  • Ends calls with "I'll think about it"
  • Accepts vague answers as progress
  • Follows up 4x and never gets a real answer
Shark closer
  • Controls the call and guides it to a decision
  • Transitions conversation to commitment naturally
  • Asks for YES or NO — then waits for the answer
  • Leaves every call with a signed contract or clear next step
The closing framework — every call
  • 1. Situation confirmed — Recap their property, timeline, and needs so they feel heard
  • 2. Problem confirmed — They've acknowledged the burden is real and the situation is urgent
  • 3. Offer presented — Clearly lay out what you can do and why it solves their problem
  • 4. Decision asked — "Does this make sense to move forward?" Then stop talking
The money line — memorize this
"Based on everything we talked about... does this make sense to move forward with?"

Say it. Then shut up. First person to speak loses. Train your closers to sit in the silence.

Roleplay drill — "No decision = fail"
Rule: Rep MUST get a YES or NO — "I'll think about it" is a failure
Seller says

"Yeah everything sounds good, I just need to think about it a little more."

Closer responds
"I get that — and I respect it. Usually when someone says that, it comes down to one of two things: either the number doesn't quite work for them, or the timing doesn't feel right. Which one is it for you?"
Today's assignment: Every call ends in a decision OR a clear, specific next step with a date and time attached. Not "I'll follow up."

Identifying the real block

"There is always ONE real reason the deal isn't closing. Find it." Sellers almost never tell you the real reason upfront.
The 5 real reasons deals stall
  • 1. Price gap — They're thinking "this number doesn't work." Ask: "Is the number the main thing standing in the way?" Make them say it out loud
  • 2. Lack of trust — They like the deal but don't fully trust you or the company yet. Share a past deal story with a real outcome
  • 3. Uncertainty — Scared to make a mistake. Simplify: "Here's exactly what happens next — no surprises"
  • 4. Other decision-makers — Spouse, sibling, attorney, or partner needs to be involved. Get everyone on the same call
  • 5. Timing — They might actually not be ready yet. Set a concrete follow-up with a specific condition attached
The key question to unlock any stall
"What's the one thing that would need to happen for you to feel comfortable moving forward today?"

Whatever they say next — that is what you work on. Not the first vague answer. The real one underneath it.

Roleplay drill — "Find the real objection"
Rule: Ask at least 2–3 questions before accepting any answer
Seller says

"I just want to think about it. I'm not sure yet."

Closer responds
"Totally fair — I don't want to rush you. Help me understand though: is it the number that's not quite working, or is it something else that doesn't feel right yet?"

You're naming possibilities so the seller just picks one — much easier than "what's wrong?" which puts them on the defensive.

Today's assignment: Don't solve fake objections. Every stall has a real cause. Find it before you try to fix it.

Price negotiation & holding the line

"Confidence closes deals. Discounts don't." Dropping immediately signals your number had no basis — and if you dropped once, you'll drop again.
The golden negotiation rule
Never give without getting something. Every price move must come with a condition: "If I can get you there, you're ready to sign today?" That's a trade — not a concession.
Lines that hold the line
  • Seller pushes for higher price: "If we were able to get closer to your number, is that something you'd move forward on today — right now?"
  • Seller says your number is too low: "Help me understand — what are you basing that number on? Let me see if that lines up with what we're working with."
  • Seller threatens to go elsewhere: "Completely your call — but before you go, what would it actually take for us to make this work?"
  • Seller wants you to match another offer: "What would you need to see from us to feel like we're the right choice — even if the other number is higher?"
  • You need to move on price: "Here's where I think I can get to — but if I do that, we need to close by [date]. Can you commit to that?"
Roleplay drill — "Don't drop first"
Rule: Closer CANNOT increase offer the moment seller asks — must question, reframe, and justify first
Seller says

"I need at least $250K. That's what my neighbor's house sold for."

Closer responds
"Got it — I appreciate you being straight with me. Help me understand that number: was that house updated inside? What condition was it in? Because the math changes a lot depending on what work it needs."

You're making them justify their number. Now they're defending their number instead of attacking yours.

Today's assignment: No emotional price drops. Every price movement must be strategic — and you must get something in return.

Killing the "think about it"

"Think about it = unresolved concern. Uncover it. Solve it. Close it." Every single stall is 100% preventable.
The 3 real reasons sellers say "think about it"
  • Price doesn't work — They're hoping more thought will magically change the math. Ask directly: "Is the number the main thing holding you back?"
  • They don't fully trust you yet — Something about you, your company, or the process still feels uncertain. Share a success story. Give them a reference from a past seller
  • They're unsure about the decision itself — They've never sold this way before. Simplify: "Here's exactly what happens from here — step by step, no surprises"
The 4 stalls and how to kill each one
"Let me think about it."
"Totally — what specifically do you need to think through? If I can answer it right now, let's do that."
Make them name the thought. Vague thinking is not a reason to let them leave.
"Just send it over and I'll look at it."
"Happy to. Before I do — what would you want to see in it to feel good about moving forward? That way I can make sure it actually answers what you're thinking through."
Turn the document request into a qualifying question. Now you know what they need.
"Call me back next week."
"Of course. Is there something specific that'll be different next week? I want to make sure when we talk, I've got what you need."
Don't just schedule the follow-up. Find out what actually changes next week.
"I need to talk to my spouse / attorney."
"Absolutely — would it help to set up a time when everyone can be on the call together? That way no one's going off second-hand info."
Don't let information get filtered through a third party. Get everyone in the room.
Roleplay drill — "No escape drill"
Rule: Seller MUST try to leave. Closer keeps them engaged — curious and helpful, never pushy.
Seller says

"Just send it over, I'll look at it and get back to you."

Closer responds
"Happy to send it — before I do, what would you want to see in it to feel good about moving forward? I want to make sure it actually answers what you're thinking through."

Run this 3 times with different stalls: "I'll think about it," "Call me later," "Send it over."

Today's assignment: Slow down every "think about it." Unpack it. Solve the real concern. Don't let motivated sellers walk out the door unaddressed.

Closing like a professional

"You don't get what you deserve. You get what you ask for." The professional close is direct, confident, and specific — not tricks or pressure.
The 3-part professional close — every time
  • Step 1 — Recap their pain: "You've got a property that needs significant work, it's been sitting vacant, and you're paying taxes on it every month..."
  • Step 2 — Recap your solution: "We can buy it as-is, handle everything, and close on your timeline. No repairs, no showings, no uncertainty."
  • Step 3 — Ask directly: "Based on everything we talked about — are you ready to move forward today?" Then stop talking. The next person to speak is not you.
Strong closes vs. weak closes
Never say these
  • "So... what do you think?"
  • "Does that sound okay to you?"
  • "Let me know when you're ready."
  • "I'll shoot you the paperwork to look at."
  • "Just give me a call if you want to move forward."
Say these instead
  • "Are you ready to move forward today?"
  • "Based on everything — does this make sense to do?"
  • "Is there anything actually stopping you from moving forward right now?"
  • "Let's get the paperwork started — what's the best email?"
  • "I'm going to send the contract over now. Let's get you closed."
The silence rule
Silence is a weapon. After asking for the decision, your closer must stop talking. Train them to count to 10 in their head if they need to. The seller will speak first — and what they say will tell you everything.
Roleplay drill — "Direct close only"
Rule: Only direct closes allowed. Weak language = stop the drill and restart.
Seller says

"Everything sounds good, I'm just not 100% sure yet."

Closer responds
"I get that — completely normal. Based on everything we discussed today, is there anything actually stopping you from moving forward right now?"

If there IS something stopping them — now you know. If there ISN'T — they move forward. Run this 5 times with different seller responses.

Today's assignment: Ask every single qualified seller for a decision. Every. Single. One. Not "let me know." Ask directly. Today.

Rapport in 60 seconds

"You don't close on the close. You close on the open." Most closers ruin the deal in the first sixty seconds and never recover.
The objective

Rapport isn't small talk. Rapport is the seller deciding, in the first minute, that you are someone they can work with — someone who listens, who isn't pushing, and who actually understands what they're going through. Get this wrong, and nothing else you learn matters.

Weak vs. shark — how you sound
Weak closer sounds like
  • Reading from a script — monotone, no emotion
  • Diving into the pitch in the first 30 seconds
  • Asking questions like an interrogation
  • Talking AT the seller, not WITH them
  • Trying to be "professional" instead of human
Shark closer sounds like
  • Conversational, calm, slightly slower than normal
  • Spending the first 2–3 minutes just listening
  • Asking questions like a curious neighbor would
  • Mirroring the seller's pace and energy
  • Sounding like a real person who happens to buy houses
The 60-second open — every call starts here
  • 1. Greet by name — "Hey John, this is Mike with 1 Hour Offers — thanks for taking my call." First name. Warm. Not corporate
  • 2. Set the frame — Tell them why you're calling and how long it'll take. "Just want to ask a few questions — takes about 5 minutes — and we'll see if it makes sense to keep talking"
  • 3. Ask about them — Before you ask about the house, ask about THEM. "How long have you owned the property? How did you end up with it?"
  • 4. Listen and mirror — Repeat back what they say in their own words. "It's been a headache" → "Sounds like it's been a real headache." They feel heard immediately
The question that builds trust instantly
"Tell me a little about the property — and what's going on with it for you."

Open-ended. Personal. They tell you the property AND the emotional context. Now you know everything you need to actually help them.

Rapport lines that work
  • Seller is hesitant on the call: "Hey — I'm not here to pressure you into anything. If it makes sense, great. If not, no hard feelings. Sound fair?"
  • Seller mentions a hard situation: "I'm really sorry to hear that. That's a lot to deal with. Take your time — walk me through what's going on."
  • You want to slow down the energy: "Let me just slow down for a second — I want to make sure I actually understand what you're looking for here."
  • Seller is suspicious / has been called a lot: "Yeah, I get it — I'm sure your phone's been ringing nonstop. I'll be quick and straight with you. No games."
  • Building common ground: "How long have you been in [city]? Same here — my [family / friend] is from over there too."
Roleplay drill — "First 60 seconds"
Rule: Rep CANNOT mention the offer, price, or company process in the first minute. Pure rapport only.
Seller says

"Yeah, what is this about? I'm busy."

Closer responds
"Hey John — it's Mike, totally understand. I'll be quick. I'm calling about the property over on Oak Street — just wanted to ask a couple questions and see if it even makes sense to keep talking. Got 5 minutes for me?"

Names them. Names yourself. Promises brevity. Asks permission. Doesn't pitch. The seller goes from defensive to neutral in 15 seconds. Run this 5 times with 5 different tones (rushed, sad, suspicious, friendly, hostile).

Coach's note — tonality beats words
Seventy percent of how a closer is perceived has nothing to do with the words — it's tone, pace, and energy. A great line said in a robotic voice loses. A simple line said with warmth wins. Drill exercise: have your rep say "Hey John, this is Mike" ten different ways — rushed, calm, friendly, tired, confident, apologetic. Ask the listener which one they'd trust. That's the version they should use every time.
Today's assignment: First 60 seconds of every call — name them, set the frame, ask about THEM. No pitching. No price. Build the human connection before the deal.

Mastering motivation discovery

"Every motivated seller has a story. Until you know the story, you have no leverage." Properties don't close deals — motivation does.
The objective

Motivation is the single biggest factor in whether a deal closes. Two sellers with the same property can be a hard pass and a layup — the difference is what's happening in their life that's pushing them to sell. The story behind the sale tells you how hard to push, how fast to move, and how to frame your offer. Weak closers ask about the property. Shark closers ask about the situation.

The 4 levels of motivation
  • 1. Tire-kicker — Curious about value, no real reason to sell. Don't spend more than 10 minutes here
  • 2. Interested — Considering selling but no urgency. Has options, may list with an agent. Move them on speed and convenience — not price
  • 3. Motivated — Has a real problem driving the sale (divorce, vacant property, repairs, taxes). Will close if the offer addresses the problem
  • 4. Desperate — Has to sell. Foreclosure, debt, distressed inheritance. Closes fast at strong margins — but requires empathy, not pressure
The question that reveals everything
"If you don't mind me asking — what's really pushing you to sell right now?"

"If you don't mind me asking" makes the question feel respectful instead of nosy. "Right now" forces a real answer. Listen carefully — the next 30 seconds are the most valuable on the call.

The 7 most common motivations — and how to use each
  • 1. Inherited property — They didn't want it, can't maintain it, want it gone. Lean into ease: "We can take it as-is so you don't have to deal with the cleanout, the repairs, or any of it"
  • 2. Divorce — They want a clean split. Speed and discretion matter more than top dollar. "We can close in 14 days and you both walk away with your share"
  • 3. Tired landlord — Bad tenants, repairs, headache. Lean into freedom: "We buy with tenants in place. You're done dealing with this on Friday"
  • 4. Relocation / job — They have a deadline. Time is the leverage. "We close on your timeline — if you need to be out by the 15th, we're out by the 15th"
  • 5. Financial distress — Behind on payments, taxes, debt. Be careful and human. "We've helped a lot of folks in this exact spot. Let me explain what we can do — no judgment"
  • 6. Health / death in family — Sensitive. Slow your pace. "I'm really sorry. Take your time. We'll work around whatever you need — and we can be flexible on closing"
  • 7. Property condition — Can't afford the repairs. As-is is your weapon. "You're not paying for repairs, you're not getting inspected. We buy it exactly how it sits"
Motivation discovery questions
  • Surface-level discovery: "How long have you owned the property? How did you end up with it?"
  • Going deeper: "If you don't mind me asking, what's really driving the sale right now?"
  • Probing urgency: "Is there a specific timeline you're working with — or is this more whenever?"
  • Probing alternatives: "Have you thought about listing it with an agent? What's holding you back from that?"
  • Confirming pain: "It sounds like [their situation] has been weighing on you for a while. Is that fair to say?"
Roleplay drill — "Find the why"
Rule: Rep cannot make an offer until they've identified WHICH of the 7 motivations is driving the sale — and named it back for confirmation.
Seller says

"Yeah, it's a 3-bed 2-bath, decent neighborhood. Just thinking about getting rid of it."

Closer responds
"Got it. If you don't mind me asking — what's really pushing you to sell right now? Is it the upkeep, a life change, the tenants — something else? Just trying to make sure I understand what would actually help you."

Listing options gives the seller permission to share. "Just trying to make sure I understand" makes it about helping them, not selling them.

Today's assignment: Identify the motivation by name (one of the 7) before you ever talk price. Match your pitch to their reason — not your script.

The follow-up system

"The fortune is in the follow-up — but only if the follow-up is intentional." Most reps stop after 2 attempts. 80% of deals close after the 4th.
The objective

Great follow-up isn't "just checking in." That's the worst phrase in wholesale. Great follow-up adds something to the conversation every single time — a new angle, a piece of value, an updated number, a relevant story. If your closer can't answer "what new value am I bringing to this contact?" — they shouldn't make the call.

Bad vs. great follow-up
Bad follow-up
  • "Hey, just checking in to see where you're at"
  • Calls without a reason or new info
  • Same message every time — voice, text, email
  • Stops after 2–3 attempts
  • Sounds desperate, not valuable
Great follow-up
  • Always brings a new angle, story, or info
  • Mixes channels — call, text, voicemail, email
  • Tied to a specific concern the seller raised
  • Keeps going — doesn't bail after a few attempts
  • Sounds confident, not chasing
The magic follow-up question
"Just want to either help you or get out of your way — where's your head at?"

Honest, low pressure, and impossible to dodge. Either gets a real answer or ends the chase. Both are wins.

Channel strategy — when to call, text, email
  • Call — Best for initial discovery, presenting an offer, hard conversations, the breakup. The seller needs to hear your tone
  • Text — Best for confirmations, quick check-ins, sending links, value adds. Faster and lower pressure than a call
  • Voicemail — Always leave one if they don't pick up. Under 20 seconds. Name + reason + ask + your number twice
  • Email — Best for sending offers in writing, comps, contracts, breakup messages they can reread later
Follow-up lines that don't sound like follow-up
  • Value-add text: "Hey John — saw this comp pop up two streets over. Thought you'd want to see it. Talk soon."
  • Story call: "John — closed a deal yesterday almost identical to yours. Took 12 days. Wanted to share how it went."
  • Offer refresh: "Hey — we've got some flex on close date if that's what was holding you up. Worth another conversation?"
  • Direct check-in: "John — want to either help you or get out of your way. Where's your head at?"
  • Breakup: "Sounds like the timing isn't right — going to stop reaching out. If anything changes, you have my number. Take care."
Roleplay drill — "The breakup call"
Rule: 7th touch. Seller has gone cold. Breakup must be firm but warm — no neediness, no guilt-tripping. Drill succeeds when seller responds (positive OR negative).
Seller says

(Seller has been silent for 14 days)

Closer responds
"Hey John — sounds like the timing isn't right on this one, so I'm going to stop reaching out. If anything changes down the road, you've got my number. Take care."

The breakup pattern works because it removes pressure. Most sellers reply because (a) the timing actually changed, or (b) they feel guilty for ghosting. Either way, you get a real answer.

Today's assignment: Every follow-up brings NEW value — a comp, a story, a piece of info, an updated offer. No "just checking in." If you can't name what you're bringing, don't make the call.

Handling tough sellers

"When the seller gets emotional, the closer who stays calm wins." When the temperature goes up, your voice goes down.
The objective

Easy sellers don't make great closers. Tough sellers do. The difference between a $50K rep and a $250K rep is what they do when the seller is angry, suspicious, sophisticated, or playing hardball with multiple offers. Tough sellers are testing you. Pass the test, and the deal closes anyway. Fail the test, and you lose a deal you could have won.

The 4 types of tough sellers
  • 1. The angry seller — Yelling, hostile, accusing wholesalers of being scammers. Their anger usually isn't about you. Don't match their energy. Lower your voice and acknowledge
  • 2. The suspicious seller — Asks pointed questions, doesn't trust the process. Win with transparency — explain everything before they have to ask
  • 3. The sophisticated seller — Investor, agent, knows the market. Will catch every weak number. Win with respect, accuracy, and not playing games
  • 4. The multi-bid seller — Has 3 offers on the table. Will use you against others. Win by being the easiest, fastest, most certain to close — not necessarily the highest
Pressure vs. composure
Under pressure — weak
  • Voice gets faster, higher, defensive
  • Talks over the seller to "win"
  • Apologizes for the offer / company / process
  • Takes accusations personally
  • Tries to argue the seller out of their feelings
Under pressure — shark
  • Voice gets slower, lower, calmer
  • Lets the seller finish — every time
  • Owns the offer with confidence, no apology
  • Hears the accusation, doesn't react to it
  • Validates feelings before solving anything
The disarm line
"I hear you. That's a fair concern. Let me explain how this actually works — and you tell me if it still feels off."

Three moves: validate, transparency, invite continued doubt. The seller goes from defensive to listening in one sentence.

Tough seller lines
  • Seller is yelling: "Hey — I hear you. I'm not here to argue. Can I ask one question, and you tell me if it's worth keeping going?"
  • Seller calls you a scammer: "I get the suspicion — there are bad actors out there. Let me be 100% transparent about how we operate, and you decide."
  • Seller says you're lowballing: "I appreciate the directness. Let me show you exactly how I got to this number — and if your math is different, walk me through yours."
  • Seller has 3 other offers: "That's great — you've got options. The question isn't who's highest. It's who's actually going to close on time. What matters more to you?"
  • Seller threatens to hang up: "Totally your call. Before you go — if there's one thing I could clear up that would change your mind, what would it be?"
Roleplay drill — "Stay flat under fire"
Rule: Trainer plays a hostile seller for 5 minutes. Rep cannot raise their voice, cannot apologize, cannot argue.
Seller says

"I'm sick of you scammers calling me. You all lowball, you all lie, and I'm about to file a complaint."

Closer responds
"I get it — and honestly, you're right that there are some bad actors in this industry. I'm not going to argue with you. If you give me 60 seconds, I'll explain exactly how we operate and what makes us different. If you still want me off the line after that, I'm gone. Fair?"

Validates the frustration without taking it personally. Asks for a tiny commitment (60 seconds). Gives the seller an off-ramp. Run 4 times — angry, suspicious, sophisticated, multi-bid.

Coach's note — the volume rule
When the seller's volume goes UP, your rep's volume must go DOWN. Anger is energy seeking matching energy — if your rep doesn't give it, the anger has nowhere to go and burns itself out in under a minute. Drill: have your rep deliver their best lines at HALF normal volume and 30% slower pace. That's the version that wins under pressure.
Today's assignment: On every difficult call this week — voice down, pace slow, no apologizing for the offer. Validate before you solve. Stay flat — win the test.

Closer's mindset & self-coaching

"You don't rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your daily habits." Average closers wait for coaching. Elite closers coach themselves.
The objective

Days 1–9 gave your closers the skills. Day 10 is what separates reps who use them for a week from those who use them for a career. Mindset is the operating system that runs every other skill on this list. The biggest difference between top and average closers isn't talent — it's that the top closer reviews calls, owns misses, drills weak spots, and treats every conversation as data to get better.

The daily closer discipline
  • 1. Pre-shift — Review yesterday's 3 hardest calls. Identify ONE thing you'll do better today. Write it on a sticky note. Stick it on the monitor
  • 2. Mid-shift — After every difficult call: 30-second debrief. "What worked? What didn't? What would I do differently?" Note it. Move on. Don't carry the loss into the next call
  • 3. Post-shift — Pick ONE call to relisten to. Score yourself honestly. Identify a single line you'll rewrite for tomorrow
  • 4. Weekly — Every Friday: review your scorecard, listen to your single best AND single worst call. Identify the gap. Drill the gap on Monday
Average vs. elite mindset
Average closer's inner voice
  • "They weren't motivated"
  • "The lead was bad"
  • "Manager will tell me what I missed"
  • "I had a rough day — not my fault"
  • "I'm just having an off week"
Elite closer's inner voice
  • "What did I miss in their motivation?"
  • "What was I supposed to do differently?"
  • "What three things will I drill before tomorrow?"
  • "Tough day = best learning. Listen back tonight"
  • "I'm one habit change away from breaking through"
The question elite closers ask themselves every day
"If I had to coach the version of me who showed up today — what would I tell them?"

Brutal honesty without self-pity. Identify the gap. Close the gap. Move on. This single habit separates elite from average more than any skill on this list.

The 5 mental traps that kill closers
  • 1. The loss hangover — A bad call ruins the next 3. Cure: 30-second reset. Stand up, breathe, name what you learned, sit down. The next call doesn't care about the last one
  • 2. The script trap — Following the script so rigidly you stop listening. Cure: throw the script for one full day a week. Force yourself to be present. Bring it back the next day, sharper
  • 3. The compare game — Comparing yourself to the top rep on the team. Cure: compare yourself to YOU one month ago. That's the only meaningful benchmark
  • 4. The money loop — Thinking about commission while on the call. Sellers feel it. Cure: focus on solving their problem. The commission follows. Reverse the order — lose the deal
  • 5. The burnout grind — Working harder, not smarter. 100 dials of bad form is worse than 30 dials of great form. Cure: protect your energy. Quality > quantity
Drill — "Self-coach the tape"
Rule: Each rep pulls ONE call from yesterday — their worst — and presents a 3-minute self-coach to the team. No defensiveness.
What it sounds like
"Right here — I asked 'so what do you think?' instead of 'are you ready to move forward today?' That's the moment I lost control. I gave them an escape hatch. Tomorrow I'm drilling 20 reps of the direct close before my first dial."

Brutal honesty + specific fix + concrete action. Reps who do this consistently become unstoppable. Rotate the team every Friday — best self-coach gets the early Monday shift pick.

The 1% rule
Don't try to fix everything tomorrow. Pick ONE thing from this 10-day system to drill this week. ONE. Get 1% better at one thing every week and in a year you're unrecognizable. Average closers try to fix five things and fix none. Elite closers fix one thing at a time and never stop fixing.
Today's assignment: Pull your worst call from this week. Listen to all of it. Identify ONE specific moment you'll fix. Drill it 20 reps before your next shift. Then do it again next week. Forever.
